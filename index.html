Final Project for Jeff Nieman

The data set examined in this project comes from a US government sponsored database for fuel economy (found at 'https://www.fueleconomy.gov/feg/download.shtml'). This is a treasure trove of information, looking at all models of vehicles from 1984 to 2017 and 83 distinct attributes. As a data scientist in the automotive industry, I focused on the highly important issue of combined mileage (both city and highway) as it provided the most realistic view of how people actually drive. This is a very important issue in the business right now, as there is considerable debate about the government mandated fuel economy requirements (known as the CAFE standards). The main story of the charts was how the mileage changes with each model year, so for the visualizations I utilized the model year as the independent variable with the combined mileage as the dependent variable. I created three interactive versions of the graph that allow the user to filter around three of the most significant variables impacting fuel economy as determined by regression analysis: vehicle class, drive type and the number of cylinders. The interactive nature of the graphs provides insight into what helps improve the gas mileage of the vehicles.

The first challenge was to clean up the data. There were a few rows with combined mileage greater than 100 mpg and some white spaces. Filtering out all rows without a 4, 6, or 8 cylinder engine anabled me to eliminate the outliers skewing the data while losing only a small percentage of the rows (38,211 down to 36,277). I performed this process in R was using the dplyr package. In addition, to make a more meaningful category of vehicle class I consolidated the values of class into cleaner buckets. For example, I combined many different Sports Utility Vehicles (with spelling and formatting variants) into one bucket called "SUV". I also consolidated the transmission types, but chose not to create an interactive graph as the p-values for many of the most common options (such as manual 5 speed) were too high. All three interactive graphs focus on attributes that pass the hypothesis test and have a clear impact on mileage. The code for the data cleansing and linear regression can be found at 'https://raw.githubusercontent.com/jeffnieman11/Data608_Final_Project/master/final.Rmd'.

There are a number of insights and conclusions seen from the data visualization. Overall, the general trend is toward improved fuel economy overall as time progresses, with more current vehicles generally improving in contrast to the past. However, it is more insightful to understand the trends through the interactivity of the graphs. For example, in the vehicle type graph several classes show clear improvement - with compacts pushing in a few cases 50 mpg as time progresses and a dramatic increase in midsize and large car mileage overall. SUV's are improving while trucks are consolidating toward the middle - discontinuing the best mileage makers while improving the worst. The graph interacting around drive type shows an increase in front-wheel drive mileage, while the rear-wheel drive improvement is far less. The four wheel drive vehicles are relatively new overall. The cylinder graph tells an interesting story. All three options show improvement. This is perhaps the cleanest slice to show an area of overall change that cuts across all vehicles. In general, while vehicle mileage of the fleet is improving, the ability to interact with the charts helps understand the areas that are driving the change.
